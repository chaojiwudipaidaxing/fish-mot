\title{A drift-proof and deployment-oriented evaluation framework for fish multi-object tracking in digital aquaculture}

\author{Anonymous Author(s)}

\begin{abstract}
This study presents an investigator-developed evaluation framework for fish multi-object tracking and counting in digital aquaculture, centered on auditable protocol design and deployment decision rules. Rather than claiming across-the-board gains, we integrate run-configuration locking, data-drift gates, manifest hash tracing, controlled detector degradation (drop/jitter), quantile stratified difficulty analysis, count stability metrics, and runtime profiling in one reproducible pipeline. On a frozen val-half snapshot (8 sequences, 1000 frames each, seeds 0/1/2), Base reports HOTA 58.232\,$\pm$\,0.304 and IDF1 64.042\,$\pm$\,0.550, while ByteTrack reports HOTA 58.431\,$\pm$\,0.291 and IDF1 64.011\,$\pm$\,0.545. The +gating/+traj/+adaptive variants expose explicit trade-offs rather than monotonic gains. Controlled degradation and bucketed diagnostics show where failures accumulate (especially low-confidence and turning regimes), while runtime profiling separates lightweight options (Base, +gating) from high-cost options (+traj, +adaptive). We translate these observations into threshold-based guidance for CPU/edge deployment under frame-rate and memory constraints. The resulting contribution is an engineering evidence framework that links fairness, stress testing, failure interpretation, and reproducible release artifacts for smart aquaculture.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Digital aquaculture relies on reliable fish trajectories and counts for biomass estimation, feeding control, and welfare monitoring. In this context, a model that is slightly better on a single benchmark but hard to reproduce or deploy can be less useful than a model with transparent limits and stable behavior. Therefore, this paper follows a route-B positioning: we do not claim universal dominance. We instead present a complete evaluation framework that links fairness, robustness diagnostics, and deployment decisions under compute constraints.

To make the claims directly testable, this work contributes:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item an auditable evaluation protocol that locks run configuration, data gates, and manifest hashes;
  \item a controlled degradation matrix (drop/jitter) plus a gating-threshold sensitivity check for robustness auditing;
  \item stratified difficulty diagnostics (occlusion/density/turn/low-confidence) to localize failure modes in ID consistency and counting;
  \item deployment decision rules derived from joint accuracy--cost evidence (FPS, memory, CPU, and count stability);
  \item a reproducibility package design for double-blind review and post-acceptance release.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related work}
\subsection{Tracking baselines and association design}
Modern online MOT systems differ mainly in association design and handling of low-confidence detections. ByteTrack associates almost every detection and is a strong and efficient baseline~\cite{bytetrack2022}. OC-SORT emphasizes observation-centric updates under motion uncertainty~\cite{ocsort2022}. BoT-SORT integrates stronger association cues and camera-motion-aware design for improved robustness~\cite{botsort2022}. These methods form the strong-baseline set in our experiments.

Beyond these recent trackers, the broader MOT lineage (e.g., SORT/DeepSORT style association pipelines~\cite{sort2016,deepsort2017}) motivates two engineering concerns in practice: metric sensitivity to detection quality and speed-memory trade-offs under deployment constraints. Our framework addresses these concerns at protocol level rather than by proposing a new universal tracker.

\subsection{Evaluation metrics and reproducible benchmarking}
HOTA explicitly balances detection and association quality and is now a standard MOT metric~\cite{hota2020}. TrackEval provides a unified implementation for metric computation and cross-method comparability~\cite{trackeval2020}. We adopt this metric/evaluator stack and extend it with multi-seed aggregation, per-sequence reporting, stratified bucket analysis, and explicit reproducibility metadata.

\subsection{Aquaculture vision, fish tracking, and counting}
Aquaculture vision has evolved from foundational computer-vision task reviews toward full digital-aquaculture stacks that integrate monitoring, management, and decision support~\cite{zion2012,fore2018,zhou2018feeding,li2020biomass,barbedo2022review,cui2025survey}. Along this trajectory, counting-oriented works have emphasized practical instrumentation and affordability, from embedded counters to vision-based hatchery/fingerling workflows and modern deep counting models~\cite{hernandez2018counter,albuquerque2019fingerlings,zhang2020fishcount}. In parallel, tracking and behavior studies have shifted toward deep models for abnormal-event detection and monitoring robustness in dense farming scenes~\cite{mei2022trackingreview,wang2022abnormal,zhao2024stereo}. 

However, most studies still report isolated task metrics (only counting, only tracking, or only behavior) and rarely connect these outcomes to reproducibility controls, stress-test protocols, and deployment constraints in one auditable pipeline. In line with CEA expectations, our novelty is an investigator-developed evaluation system (drift-proof + stress-tested + deployment-aware), not a first-use re-packaging of off-the-shelf methods.

\section{Methods: investigator-developed evaluation framework}
\subsection{Drift-proof reproducibility core}
The framework starts from a single run configuration (\texttt{run\_config.json}) that locks split, frame budget, seeds, degradation parameters, gating threshold, and data root. Data drift is prevented by an explicit MOT-root policy and a data gate. Outputs are routed into fixed subdirectories (tables, runtime, stratified, paper assets, release), while manifest hashing records provenance of both inputs and outputs.

\subsection{Controlled degradation protocol}
We evaluate robustness under controlled detection degradation using drop rate and bounding-box jitter. This protocol quantifies how rapidly tracking identity and matching quality degrade as sensor/detector quality worsens, which is central for aquaculture edge deployments where lighting/turbidity conditions vary.

\subsection{Stratified difficulty diagnostics}
To expose failure mechanisms beyond global means, we run quantile-based stratified evaluation over occlusion, density, turning, and low-confidence scores. Each bucket reports F1, IDSW, and counting errors, enabling direct identification of difficulty regimes that trigger association instability.

\subsection{Count stability and runtime profiling}
We augment MOT metrics with count stability indicators (CountMAE/CountRMSE/CountVar/CountDrift) and runtime profiles (FPS, memory peak, normalized CPU). This makes it possible to build threshold-based deployment rules rather than relying on one-score ranking.

\section{Experimental setup}
\subsection{Frozen configuration and data}
Configuration is locked in the frozen release package: split=val-half, MOT root=\texttt{data/mft25\_mot\_full}, max frames=1000, seeds=[0, 1, 2], drop rate=0.2, jitter=0.02, and gating threshold=2000.0. The evaluated val-half bundle contains eight sequences and 8000 evaluated frames in total.

\subsection{Methods and fairness protocol}
We evaluate Base, +gating, +traj, +adaptive, and strong baselines ByteTrack/OC-SORT/BoT-SORT. Fairness rule: all trackers consume the same detection source and are evaluated with the same split/frame budget and TrackEval pipeline. Reporting uses multi-seed mean$\pm$std when available.

\subsection{Research questions}
RQ1: How do core variants and strong baselines compare on global metrics?\\
RQ2: What robustness trend appears under controlled degradation?\\
RQ3: Which difficulty buckets dominate ID switches and counting errors?\\
RQ4: Which methods satisfy deployment budgets on FPS/memory/CPU?

\section{Discussion: deployment guideline}
The results support threshold-based deployment decisions.
Gating-threshold selection is not ad hoc in this setup: the 1000/2000/4000 sweep falls in a plateau with identical +gating metrics, so using 2000 reflects stable behavior rather than single-point tuning.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{If target is at least 30 FPS and memory budget is up to 600 MB}: Base and +gating both satisfy constraints comfortably (822.8/1182.2 FPS; 549.0/548.4 MB).
  \item \textbf{If heavier compute is acceptable} (e.g., server-side analysis): +traj/+adaptive can be considered, but their speed-memory-CPU costs are high and should be justified by task-specific value.
  \item \textbf{Risk-aware monitoring}: low-confidence and turning buckets show strong IDSW growth (e.g., low-confidence (mid)), so online confidence alarms or conservative association policies are recommended in those conditions.
\end{itemize}

This closes an engineering decision loop from metrics to deployment policy: global benchmark + failure bucket diagnostics + resource budget.

\section{Limitations}
This study has several limitations. First, the evaluation scope is limited to the current val-half split and detector condition; broader domain shifts (different farms, sensors, water conditions) are not fully covered. Second, our variants do not show universal global metric gains in this frozen run, so conclusions are intentionally framed as trade-offs. Third, runtime profiling is collected on the current host and does not yet include dedicated edge hardware benchmarks. Fourth, although reproducibility metadata is strong, data sharing may still be constrained by upstream licensing.

\section{Conclusion and future work}
We presented a CEA-oriented, drift-proof evaluation framework for fish MOT and counting that prioritizes robustness-cost analysis, failure transparency, and deployment guidance over unsupported winner-takes-all claims. The framework combines fair baseline comparison, controlled degradation, stratified diagnostics, count stability, and runtime profiling within one auditable pipeline.

Future work for the smart-aquaculture PhD direction includes: (i) multi-sensor fusion with environmental signals (e.g., dissolved oxygen and water temperature), (ii) anomaly behavior recognition with trajectory-context coupling, (iii) cross-pond/domain generalization, and (iv) online updating/calibration strategies under long-term deployment drift.
